Welcome to the Cinema Society Website!

This website explores the evolution of film and its impact on other films. We accomplish this goal by encouraging discussion about film and allowing visitors to submit their own films to our website for our consideration.

Every month we take user submitted films and add them to the home page for everyone to view!

Early Influential Films

The Great Train Robbery (1903)

Considered one of the first narrative films, "The Great Train Robbery" directed by Edwin S. Porter is a groundbreaking work that introduced storytelling to cinema.

The Birth of a Nation (1915)

Directed by D.W. Griffith, this controversial film is known for its technical innovations but criticized for its racist content. It remains influential in the history of cinema. It is still infamously known for being the first film shown in the White House.

Nosferatu (1922)

Directed by F. W. Murnau, "Nosferatu" is an iconic silent horror film. It is an unauthorized adaptation of Bram Stoker's "Dracula" and is known for its eerie atmosphere and expressionist style.

Metropolis (1927)

Fritz Lang's "Metropolis" is a landmark in science fiction cinema, featuring groundbreaking special effects and exploring themes of industrialization and class struggle.

Citizen Kane (1941)

Orson Welles' "Citizen Kane" revolutionized filmmaking by taking great chances. This includes revealing the story in the opening act and utilizes an unreliable narrotor to tell it's story. It is still considered by most the greatest film ever made.

2001: A Space Odyssey (1968)

Directed by Stanley Kubrick, this science fiction masterpiece is known for its groundbreaking visual effects and profound exploration of human evolution.
